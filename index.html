Key Economic Snapshot

$207B GDP 2025 IMF
2.0% Growth 2026 IMF Feb 2026
7.5% Inflation 2026 IMF
$52.4B External Financing 2025 Min. of Finance
Ukraine's economy remains in wartime equilibrium. Growth slowed to an estimated 2% in 2025 from 2.9% in 2024. The IMF projects 2% growth for 2026 under continued war conditions. Key constraints: labor shortage (11% unemployment), external financing dependency (~$52B annually), and elevated public debt (108–122% of GDP). External financing architecture remains robust: $136.5B committed over 2026–2029 (IMF EFF), with 70% of 2025 support flowing from frozen Russian assets via the G7 ERA mechanism.
GDP Recovery Trajectory: Ukraine's economy contracted 28.8% in 2022, rebounded 5.5% in 2023, and grew 2.9% in 2024. Real GDP in 2025 is estimated at approximately 20% below pre-war (2021) levels. Recovery is driven by private consumption, defense-related manufacturing, and sustained international financial support.
Source: World Bank, IMF Country Report Feb 2026

Macroeconomic Foundations

Inflation & Monetary Policy

Inflation peaked at 15.9% in May 2025, driven by food price shocks and labor cost pass-through. NBU raised the policy rate to 15.5% in March 2025, where it has held. As of November 2025, inflation eased to 9.3%. The NBU forecasts 7.5% inflation at end-2026, targeting 5% by end-2027. Core inflation and services prices remain elevated, reflecting persistent labor market tightness (nominal private-sector wages growing ~24% y/y).
Source: National Bank of Ukraine, World Bank MPO Oct 2025

Labor Market Constraints

Labour shortage is the single most binding production constraint identified by the IMF, World Bank, and NBU. Unemployment rate: ~10.2% (down from post-invasion peaks but still elevated). Mobilisation and emigration (~6–8 million displaced) have removed a significant share of workingage males from the labour force. This structural constraint persists regardless of military outcome.
Source: IMF Country Report Feb 2026

Current Account & Trade

Current account deficit widening: Ukraine's current account moved from a +5.1% surplus in 2022 (import compression) to a -7.2% deficit in 2024, and is estimated at -16.1% of GDP in 2025. The IMF projects -18.9% in 2026, driven by gas and military equipment imports. Goods exports fell ~5% y/y in H1 2025 following the June 2025 expiration of EU Autonomous Trade Measures and transition to the DCFTA 2.0 quota regime. The EU accounts for >50% of Ukraine's goods trade (€67.2B in 2024).
Source: World Bank, EU Commission Trade

External Financing & Reconstruction

2025 Financing Achievement: $52.4B

$37.9B G7 ERA Loans (70%) G7 / Frozen Assets
$12.1B EU Ukraine Facility EU Commission
$0.91B IMF EFF IMF
The G7's Extraordinary Revenue Acceleration (ERA) mechanism is the critical lynchpin. These loans are repaid from profits on ~$210 billion in immobilized Russian sovereign assets held in Belgium's Euroclear. Total ERA envelope: $50B for 2025–2026. The EU has delivered its full share (€18.1B); remaining funds disbursed in 2026. This structure dramatically reduces Ukraine's sovereign counterparty risk for properly structured transactions.

Reconstruction Needs: $588B (RDNA5, Feb 2026)

Total reconstruction and recovery needs: nearly $588 billion over the next decade (as of 31 Dec 2025). This is approximately 3× Ukraine's estimated 2025 nominal GDP. Direct damage reached $195 billion by end-2025 (up from $176B in Feb 2025). At least $20 billion in needs have already been met since Feb 2022 through urgent repairs and early recovery. For 2026 alone, the government plans $15.25 billion in recovery programs, of which ~34% is already secured.
Source: World Bank / RDNA5 Feb 2026

Financing Infrastructure: IMF EFF 2026–2029

New IMF Extended Fund Facility (approved Feb 2026): SDR 5.94B (~$8.1B, 295% of quota), 48-month arrangement. Total 4-year international support package: $136.5 billion, with $52B financing gap identified for 2026. Composition: ERA loans (G7), EU facilities (€50B Ukraine Facility + €90B bridge loan for 2026–27), World Bank DPLs, bilateral donors, and IMF. The program targets macro stability, debt sustainability, governance reform, and EU accession alignment.
Source: IMF Country Report & EFF Arrangement Feb 2026

Banking Sector & Credit Infrastructure

State-owned banks dominate: 52.2% of total banking sector assets (Aug 2025), >60% of household deposits. Asset quality concerns persist: Non-performing loans at 35.5% for business loans, 14% for individual loans. State-owned banks have highest NPLs (38.5%) vs private Ukrainian (9.9%) and foreign-capital (9.5%) banks.

Lending accelerating despite wartime conditions: Net hryvnia corporate loan portfolios grew >30% y/y. The NBU's 5-7-9 Programme (preferential lending) supports ~one third of outstanding hryvnia loans. State-supported mortgages via "eOselya" cover >95% of mortgage market. Capital markets development urgent: The EBRD is leading development of vertically integrated capital markets infrastructure (trading, clearing, settlement, custody) to attract reconstruction capital.
Source: NBU Financial Stability Reports, OECD March 2026

Risk & Constraint Assessment

⚠ Fiscal Unsustainability & Debt Trajectory

Public debt projected at 108–122% of GDP by 2026. Fiscal deficit (excl. grants) at ~19–21% of GDP. Domestic revenue covers only ~41% of GDP in expenditure terms; the remainder relies on external financing. Domestic revenue mobilisation reforms underway but face structural barriers (large informal economy, narrow formal base).

IMF Feb 2026, World Bank

⚠ State-Owned Bank Dominance & Shallow Capital Markets

52.2% of banking assets in state hands; >60% of deposits. Capital markets underdeveloped. Mortgage lending 96% state-subsidised; private credit thin. NPL secondary market nascent. This structural constraint pre-dates 2022 and cannot be resolved until state bank privatisation, NPL market development, and capital market infrastructure mature.

OECD March 2026, NBU

⚠ FX Controls & Capital Repatriation

Capital controls remain under martial law. Hryvnia on managed float; NBU holds $46B reserves (5 months of imports). Foreign investor dividend repatriation restricted. The NBU is implementing staged easing strategy, but progress depends on macroeconomic and security preconditions. For Danish firms, repatriation risk is real and may persist several years post-conflict.

NBU FX Strategy July 2023 (ongoing)

⚠ Energy Infrastructure Vulnerability

Russian attacks destroyed or damaged ~70% more energy assets in 2024 vs RDNA3 baseline. Power generation, transmission, distribution, and district heating all compromised. Energy insecurity drives inflation (electricity cost pass-through), disrupts production, adds backup-power costs. The EBRD deployed €1.2B+ in energy support in 2025 alone.

EBRD Feb 2026, RDNA5

⚠ Governance, Anti-Corruption & Judicial Gaps

IMF flagged "weakening political buy-in for governance reforms." In July 2025, legislation was enacted that stripped key anti-corruption enforcement powers — a structural benchmark breach. Ukraine's anti-corruption infrastructure (NABU, SAPO, NACP) remains a work in progress. EU accession conditionality is the strongest external lever; progress is real but uneven.

IMF Country Report No. 26/58 Feb 2026

⚠ Procurement Integrity in Donor Flows

The scale of donor-funded reconstruction ($588B) creates governance risk. The IMF explicitly flagged public investment management weaknesses and the need for comprehensive PIM frameworks. Firms engaged in donor-funded contracts face compliance and reputational risk if procurement integrity fails.

IMF 2025 2026

⚠ War Duration & Military Uncertainty

IMF, World Bank, and EBRD identify war duration as the primary risk. A downside scenario (longer war, worse peace terms) would require $162.9B in cumulative support vs $148.8B baseline. Energy infrastructure attacks intensified in late 2024–2025. The new IMF EFF explicitly notes that "risks to the downside scenario are exceptionally high."

IMF Feb 2026

Business Opportunity Mapping

For Danish firms, the optimal entry strategies leverage multilateral platforms and structured de-risking mechanisms rather than direct bilateral exposure.

1. War-Risk Insured Trade & Project Finance (EIFO Archetype)

Danish firms exporting goods/services to Ukraine (equipment, engineering, energy technology) can structure transactions with EIFO cover atop EBRD/IFC guarantees. The EBRD operates a War Risk Insurance Facility whereby Ukrainian insurers issue war risk policies backed by EBRD's Ukraine Recovery and Reconstruction Guarantee Facility. Entry mechanism: Apply as contractor, sub-contractor, or co-investor under active MDB programmes (DRIVE, SURGE, RISE). Register on World Bank/EBRD procurement portals; engage Danish Trade Council for warm introductions. EBRD

2. Export Credit & Guarantee Schemes

Multiple active guarantee schemes create entry points: (1) EBRD Trade Finance Programme (guarantees on Ukrainian bank payment risk); (2) IFC war-risk instruments for private sector trade; (3) EU Ukraine Facility TA windows; (4) World Bank DRIVE, SURGE, RISE programmes. Entry mechanism: Direct engagement with EIFO; coordinate with established local banking partners. IFC

3. Banking, Payments & Fintech

Ukraine's digital banking infrastructure is advanced (71 banks, heavily digitalised payments). Opportunities: (a) Correspondent banking with Ukrainian banks as reconstruction and EU trade volumes increase; (b) NPL portfolio advisory and secondary market participation (35.5% business loan NPLs); (c) Capital markets infrastructure (EBRD-led vertically integrated clearing/custody holding); (d) Fintech partnerships aligned with EU standards. Why it matters: The NBU and IMF are actively building an NPL secondary market. NBU

4. Insurance & Reinsurance

Ukraine's insurance sector restructured post-invasion (23 insurers exited; Russian-connected firms removed). The EBRD's War Risk Insurance Facility creates structured entry for international reinsurers. Danish or Nordic reinsurance firms can access EBRD guarantees to back war-risk covers written by Ukrainian primary insurers. Post-conflict, significant market for property reconstruction, mortgage, and professional indemnity (audit/advisory) insurance. EBRD

5. Fund-of-Fund & Equity Vehicles

The Ukraine Investment Framework and associated vehicles provide structured co-investment mechanisms. The IFC estimates the private sector could fund ~one third of $588B reconstruction needs (~$170B over a decade). Danish institutional investors (pension funds, alternative managers) can access via EBRD/IFC co investment platforms. New investment fund legislation being drafted (IMF 2025) will provide EU-standard legal infrastructure. IFC

6. Audit, Advisory & Compliance Services

Donor-funded programmes require international-standard audit, procurement oversight, and compliance services. The EU Ukraine Facility's "money-for-reforms" structure generates sustained demand for: public investment management, customs and tax administration reform, EU acquis alignment (DCFTA 2.0 compliance assessment, agri-food standards), anti-corruption audits, financial market infrastructure. Entry mechanism: Monitor EU procurement portal (TED); form consortia with established local partners; leverage Danish institutional expertise. OECD

7. Agri-Tech & Food Standards Alignment

The DCFTA 2.0 (entered force Oct 2025) conditions additional EU market access for Ukrainian agri-food exports on alignment with EU production standards (animal welfare, pesticides, veterinary medicines, traceability). This creates structured multi-year demand for Danish agri-technology and food standards expertise. Why it works: Denmark's strengths in agri-tech, veterinary services, and food processing technology align perfectly with Ukraine's needs. EU's share of Ukraine's agrifood exports rose to 52% in 2024 vs 28% in 2021. EU Commission

Practical Entry Strategies

For Firms Seeking Immediate Near-Term Engagement

✓ Advisory & Compliance Services (Lowest Risk)
Bid for EU Ukraine Facility TA mandates in customs reform, tax digitisation, EU acquis alignment, agri-food standards. Multi-year, stable revenue. Zero capital at risk. Danish expertise in public finance, veterinary standards, and food safety is directly applicable. Timeline: Engagement feasible within 6–12 months.
✓ Agri-Tech & Standards Alignment (Medium Risk, High Strategic Fit)
Supply technology and expertise for DCFTA 2.0 compliance — aligning Ukrainian agri-food production with EU standards. Partner with Ukrainian agri-processing groups; engage Danish Agri & Food Council's Ukraine-focused programmes. Timeline: Multi-year engagement; disbursements tied to export volumes.

For Firms with Capital & Risk Appetite

✓ EIFO-Covered Trade Finance
Use EIFO's export credit guarantees to cover Ukrainian buyer credit risk on Danish goods/services exports. The external financing architecture ($40–52B/year) makes Ukrainian sovereign counterparty risk manageable. Engage EIFO directly; structure through EBRD Trade Finance Programme counterpart Ukrainian banks. Timeline: Transaction structuring: 3–6 months.
✓ Financial Sector Advisory & NPL Secondary Market
Target the emerging NPL secondary market and capital markets infrastructure development. 35.5% NPL ratio in business lending; active IMF/NBU push to develop NPL market. Advisory on NPL workout, portfolio valuation, or securitisation framework design. Timeline: Deal flow expected 2026–2027.
